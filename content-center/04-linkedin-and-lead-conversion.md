# PART 1 — LINKEDIN GROWTH STRATEGY

## The core idea

You are not "selling software." You are showing nonprofit leaders that the opportunity around their mission is **already there and mappable** — and that Anansi Atlas is the instrument that makes it visible. Content shows the map. Comments start the relationship. DMs open the pilot conversation. The website link closes.

Right now the campaign is **product-forward**: lead with the Opportunity Web Snapshot, the platform, and the pilot — not Marcus's founder story. Marcus's account is the *voice* delivering product-forward content, not a personal-brand-story engine (yet).

---

## 1. Posting frequency & channel split

| Channel | Frequency | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Marcus Scott (personal)** | 3–4x / week | The engine. 90% of reach and all relationship-building happens here. People buy from people. |
| **Anansi Atlas (business page)** | 2–3x / week | The credibility anchor. Where a warm lead lands to verify you're real. Repost/adapt Marcus's best posts + product visuals. |

Rule of thumb: **every business-page post gets re-shared or re-authored from Marcus's account** with a personal framing line on top. The page proves legitimacy; the person drives action.

Post to Marcus mid-morning on weekdays (Tue/Wed/Thu are strongest for nonprofit-leader audiences). Never post and disappear — the first 60 minutes of replying to comments is what the algorithm rewards.

## 2. What goes where — personal vs. business page

### Marcus Scott (personal) — the relationship + insight feed
- **Insight posts:** "3 places nonprofits leave partnership money on the table," "Why grant search ≠ opportunity mapping."
- **Product-in-context posts:** a clean Opportunity Web screenshot with a caption explaining the *thinking*, not the features. ("This is one org's mission at the center. Every pill is a real, named route to capital or partnership we mapped around it.")
- **Snapshot walkthroughs:** a carousel of a real (or representative) Snapshot — summary → 30-day action plan → funder pathways → risks & gaps.
- **Pilot invitations** (soft, ~1 in 5 posts): "We're taking 20 founding orgs into the pilot. Comment 'Atlas' and I'll send details."
- **Comment-bait questions:** "Nonprofit leaders — what's the #1 opportunity you *know* is out there but can't get to? Drop it below."

### Anansi Atlas (business page) — the proof + product showcase
- Polished product UI showcases (Opportunity Web orb, Snapshot pages, Dashboard "What To Do Next" card).
- Pilot announcement + founding-rate details (the page is allowed to be more overtly commercial).
- Short explainer of the six nodes: **Funders · Partners · Government · Resources · Readiness · Pathways.**
- Social proof once you have it: pilot org quotes, "X founding seats left."

**Never on the personal page:** desperate "please sign up" posts, long founder origin stories (not yet), hype/AI-magic claims, charity framing.

## 3. Comments — your #1 growth lever

More pilot conversations will come from **your comments on other people's posts** than from your own posts. This is the highest-leverage, least-spammy channel.

**Daily comment routine (20–30 min):**
1. Comment on 5–8 posts from nonprofit EDs, development directors, program officers, and sector commentators.
2. Add a genuinely useful thought — never "Great post!" and never pitch.
3. Let your profile + prior content do the selling. Curious people click through.

**Example comments (steal these):**

> On a post about a nonprofit's grant win:
> "Congrats — this is the part people miss: the grant is one node. The partners and government pathways that open *because* of a win like this are usually worth more than the grant itself. Nicely done."

> On a post about funding being hard to find:
> "The funding is rarely the hard part — it's that it's scattered across portals, relationships, and local knowledge no one has time to map. When you lay it out around a single mission it's surprising how much is actually reachable."

> On a sector-trends post:
> "This tracks. The orgs I see pulling ahead aren't chasing more opportunities — they're getting clear on which 3 to actually move on. Focus beats volume every time in this world."

Notice: **no link, no pitch, no product name required.** Value first. The click-through does the work.

---

## 4. Engaging nonprofit leaders (relationship, not prospecting)

- **Follow + engage before you ever DM.** Warm up over days, not minutes.
- **React to their wins publicly.** A thoughtful comment on a leader's post is worth more than 10 cold DMs.
- **Speak their reality:** capacity constraints, board pressure, restricted funding, "we know the opportunity is there but can't get to it." Show you understand the job, not just the sale.
- **Never open with the product.** Open with their mission.

---

## 5. Inviting to the pilot without sounding spammy

The anti-spam formula: **relevance + specificity + an easy out.**

- Tie the invite to something *they* said or posted.
- Be specific about what the pilot is and what it costs (naming the real $150/mo founding rate signals confidence, not desperation — free things feel cheap and suspicious).
- Always give a graceful exit ("no worries if the timing's off").
- **Invite, don't chase.** One good invite + one follow-up. Then let it rest.

Scarcity is real here — use it honestly: "We're only taking 20 founding orgs." That's a fact, not a tactic.

---

## 6. Using the website link (given algorithm link-suppression)

LinkedIn suppresses reach on posts with outbound links in the body. So:

- **Never put the apply link in the post body.** Put a CTA instead: *"Comment 'Atlas' or DM me and I'll send the details."*
- **Put the link in the first comment** if you want it discoverable, or edit it in after the post gains traction.
- **The link's real home is the DM and the profile.** Send `https://anansiatlas.com/anansi-atlas/` privately, after interest is established.
- Put the link in Marcus's **Featured section** and the business page's **CTA button / About**, so profile-viewers find it without you posting it.

**Sequence:** content (no link) → comment/DM interest → conversation → *then* link.

---

## 7. Turning commenters into pilot conversations

Every comment on your post is a raised hand. Workflow:

1. **Reply publicly** with value (keeps the thread alive + algorithm-friendly).
2. **React to their comment** so they get a notification.
3. **Within 24h, open a DM** referencing the exact comment.

See Part 2 for the exact DM scripts.

---

## 8. Using DMs after engagement

DMs work **only after** a like/comment/profile view — never truly cold. The DM references the specific interaction so it never reads like a blast. First DM = conversation-opener, **not** a pitch. First reply from them = you can introduce the pilot. Link comes only once they say "tell me more."

---

## 9. Following up with warm leads

- **One value-add follow-up**, then rest. Never more than 2 unanswered messages.
- Follow-ups carry *new* value (a relevant Snapshot example, a funder-mapping insight), never "just checking in."
- Warm leads who went quiet: re-engage in **2–3 weeks** with a fresh angle or a scarcity update ("down to the last few founding seats").

# PART 2 — LEAD CONVERSION SYSTEM (with real scripts)

## Lead-stage model

| Stage | Definition | Next action |
|---|---|---|
| **0 — Cold** | No interaction yet | Engage their content first |
| **1 — Engaged** | Liked/commented/viewed profile | Send opener DM |
| **2 — In Conversation** | Replied to a DM | Qualify + surface pilot |
| **3 — Interested** | Asked for details / said "tell me more" | Send link + Snapshot context |
| **4 — Applying** | Clicked link / on the apply page | Offer walkthrough, remove friction |
| **5 — Founding Partner** | Signed up | Onboard, ask for referral |
| **X — Nurture** | Went cold at any stage | Re-engage in 2–3 weeks |

### Tracking table columns
`Name | Org | Role | Stage (0–5/X) | Source (post/comment/profile view) | Last touch date | Next action | Next action date | Notes (mission, pain, objection)`

Keep it in a simple sheet. Update every touch.

---

## Scenario A — Someone LIKES a post

A like is a light signal. Don't DM immediately. Instead:
1. Like one of *their* recent posts + leave one thoughtful comment.
2. If they're a clear fit (nonprofit ED/dev director), send a soft opener 1–2 days later:

> "Hey [First name] — saw you connected with my post on [topic]. I work with nonprofit leaders on mapping the opportunity around their mission — funders, partners, government pathways, the whole web. What kind of work does [Org] focus on?"

Lead with *their* mission. No pitch.

---

## Scenario B — Someone COMMENTS on a post

Highest-intent signal. Move within 24h.

**Step 1 — public reply:**
> "Really good point, [First name]. That gap between 'the opportunity exists' and 'we can actually reach it' is exactly what we map. Appreciate you jumping in."

**Step 2 — DM opener:**
> "Thanks for the comment on the [topic] post, [First name] — it stuck with me. Curious: for [Org], is the harder part *finding* aligned opportunities or *prioritizing* the ones you already know about? That distinction is basically the whole game for the orgs I work with."

Open a real question. Their answer tells you the pain and moves them to Stage 2.

---

## Scenario C — Someone VIEWS your profile

They're curious but haven't raised a hand. Warm, low-pressure:

> "Hi [First name] — noticed you stopped by my profile. No agenda at all. I help mission-driven orgs see the full landscape of funders, partners, and pathways around their work. If that's ever useful for [Org], happy to show you what it looks like. Either way, glad to connect."

---

## DM variants

### Cold-ish (Stage 1, minimal prior interaction)
> "Hi [First name] — I came across [Org] and your work on [mission area] caught my attention. I run Anansi Atlas, a platform that maps the web of opportunity around a nonprofit's mission — aligned funders, potential partners, government pathways, readiness gaps, all in one view. Would it be worth showing you what that looks like for an org like yours?"

### Warm (Stage 2, they've engaged/replied)
> "Love that context, [First name] — [reflect their mission back] is exactly the kind of work where the opportunity map gets interesting. We're taking 20 founding organizations into a pilot right now: you get your own Opportunity Web Snapshot, a 45-minute walkthrough, and full platform access. Founding orgs lock in $150/mo for life. Want me to send the details so you can see if it's a fit?"

### Re-engagement (Stage X, went quiet)
> "Hey [First name] — circling back because we're down to the last few founding seats and [Org] kept coming to mind given your work on [mission area]. No pressure at all — but if mapping your funder + partner landscape is still something you'd want this year, now's the moment to lock the founding rate. Want the details?"

---

## When to send the website link

**Only at Stage 3+** — after they say "tell me more," "send details," or ask about price.

> "Here's everything on the Founding Atlas Partners pilot — what's included, the founding rate, and how to apply: https://anansiatlas.com/anansi-atlas/
>
> Short version: you join at $150/mo (locked for life for the first 20 orgs), and your Opportunity Web Snapshot + 45-min walkthrough + full platform access come with it. No per-report fees, no usage limits, cancel anytime. Happy to walk you through a Snapshot live before you decide — want to grab 20 minutes?"

Note: the Snapshot is framed as **included**, never free.

---

## Inviting them to apply

Make applying the obvious next step, with a human on the other side:

> "If [Org] feels like a fit, the founding cohort is the way to get in — it locks the rate and gets you a Snapshot built on your actual mission. You can apply here: https://anansiatlas.com/anansi-atlas/. Want me to save you one of the founding seats while you look it over?"

---

## Multi-touch follow-up sequence (with timing)

For a Stage 2–3 lead who's gone quiet. Each touch adds value; stop the moment they reply or after Touch 4.

| Touch | Timing | Message |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Day 0 | (Initial warm DM / link — see above) |
| **2** | Day 3 | "Quick one, [First name] — here's what a real Snapshot looks like: it opens with a 30-day action plan, then funder pathways, partner pathways, and your readiness gaps. This is the part orgs tell me they wish they'd had years ago. Worth a look for [Org]?" |
| **3** | Day 7 | "No worries if the timing's not right, [First name]. One thing worth flagging: founding seats are capped at 20 and the $150/mo-for-life rate goes away after. If you want me to hold one while you decide, just say the word." |
| **4** | Day 14 | "I'll leave it here so I'm not cluttering your inbox, [First name]. If mapping [Org]'s opportunity landscape becomes a priority, my door's open — just reply and we'll pick it back up. Rooting for the work you're doing regardless." |
| **Re-engage** | +2–3 weeks | (Use the re-engagement DM above with a fresh scarcity or Snapshot angle.) |

Touch 4 is a graceful, warm exit — it keeps the door open and never sounds desperate.

---

## Daily operating rhythm (put it on repeat)

1. **Post or reshare** (Marcus 3–4x/wk, page 2–3x/wk).
2. **Reply to every comment** on your posts within the first hour.
3. **20–30 min of value comments** on nonprofit leaders' posts.
4. **Work the tracker:** every Stage-1+ lead gets its next action taken on schedule.
5. **Send links only at Stage 3+.** Everything before that is conversation.

The whole system: **content earns attention → comments build trust → DMs open the door → the pilot invite (with the real $150 founding rate) closes 19–20 founding partners.**
