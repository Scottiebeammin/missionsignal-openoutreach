## The Product — What You're Selling

**Anansi Atlas** delivers an **Opportunity Web Snapshot**: a consulting-grade intelligence brief that maps the full ecosystem around a nonprofit's mission. It surfaces:
- Aligned grant funders (foundations, government, corporate)
- Strategic partnership opportunities
- Community resources and technical assistance
- Funding readiness gaps and risks
- 30-day action plan with specific next steps

**The core insight Anansi Atlas sells:** Opportunity is not missing — it's scattered. Most nonprofits are sitting inside a web of aligned funders and partners they've never seen. Anansi Atlas makes that web visible.

**What it is NOT:** A grant database. A promise of funding. A generic resource list. A report the client has to interpret themselves.

## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

### Who to Target
- **Title:** Executive Director, Development Director, Co-Founder, Program Director
- **Org budget:** $250K–$5M (too small = no capacity to act on insights; too large = have in-house capacity)
- **Stage:** Growth-stage nonprofits trying to diversify funding, build partnerships, or scale programs
- **Trigger signals** (prioritize orgs showing any of these):
  - Recently launched a new program
  - Announced a capital campaign or matching grant
  - Hiring a Development Director for the first time
  - New ED in the last 12 months
  - Just received a major grant (now need to prove impact to renew)
  - Filed a 990 showing 80%+ revenue from one source (dangerous concentration)
  - Active LinkedIn presence talking about funding or partnerships

### Focus Areas with Highest Product-Market Fit
These sectors have the most active funder ecosystems and the most fragmented org intelligence:
1. Workforce Development — dense funder ecosystem (DOL, foundations, CDFI)
2. Youth Development — SEL, after-school, mentorship grant pools are huge
3. Housing & Homelessness — CDBG, HUD, local continua of care are navigable
4. Mental Health — expanding post-COVID; organizations are overwhelmed
5. Education — community schools, literacy, early childhood well-funded
6. Reentry / Justice-Involved — emerging funder attention, orgs are under-resourced on development

## IRS/NTEE Context for Research

When researching a prospect via their 990 or Candid profile, look for:
- **NTEE Code** — the IRS classification that tells you their primary mission (e.g., B60 = Adult Education, E30 = Mental Health)
- **Revenue concentration** — % from government vs. foundation vs. individual gives you their vulnerability
- **Program service revenue** — nonprofits earning fees have a different pitch than purely grant-dependent ones
- **Recent grant history** — if Candid shows recent foundation grants, those funders' peer networks are warm prospects too
- **Board composition** — corporate board members signal corporate partnership potential

Public 990 data is at ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer (nonprofits.propublica.org) — always check before outreach.

## Email Outreach Framework (GLF Angle)

Anansi Atlas uses the **GLF (Got, Lead, Follow)** framework for cold outreach:

**Structure:**
1. **Hook (1-2 lines):** Specific observation about *their* org that shows you did real research — reference their mission, a recent initiative, or a gap you spotted in their 990
2. **The shift (1-2 lines):** Name the specific problem Anansi Atlas solves for orgs like theirs
3. **The offer (1 line):** Concrete, low-friction next step — not "let's hop on a call" but "here's what your Snapshot would show"
4. **CTA (1 line):** Single link, single action

**Tone:** Direct but warm. Not salesy. Not academic. Write like a peer who has done homework on their mission — not a vendor pitching a product.

**Length:** Under 150 words for cold email. Under 80 words for LinkedIn DM.

**Never say:**
- "I wanted to reach out" (passive, weak)
- "Hope this finds you well" (filler)
- "We help nonprofits find funding" (generic)
- "Let me know if you have questions" (weak close)
- "I'd love to learn more about your work" (puts labor on them)

**Always personalize:**
- Reference their specific focus area, not "nonprofits in general"
- Reference a real funding challenge or gap specific to their sector
- Name the Snapshot — don't just say "our platform"

## Objection Handling

**"We already use GrantStation / Candid / Foundation Directory."**
Those are funder databases — they show you who exists. Anansi Atlas shows you who is *aligned to your specific mission* and *what readiness gaps are blocking you from winning them*. The Snapshot also covers partners, government pathways, and risks — not just grant lists.

**"We don't have budget right now."**
The founding cohort pricing is designed for orgs at your stage. And the first thing the Snapshot tells you is where your best near-term funding opportunities are — it's designed to pay for itself.

**"Our ED is really busy."**
The intake takes 10 minutes. We do the analysis. You get a brief, not a project.

**"We have a consultant who handles this."**
Your consultant knows your org. Anansi Atlas maps the external ecosystem they may not have full visibility into — it's a tool that makes your consultant more effective, not a replacement.

**"How is this different from hiring a grant writer?"**
Grant writers write applications for funders you've already identified. Anansi Atlas identifies which funders are worth pursuing in the first place — and which partnerships and government pathways complement the grant strategy.

## Post-Outreach Sequence Logic

After initial outreach, follow this timing:
- Day 0: First email/DM
- Day 4: First follow-up — add one new data point (a funder they might not know about, a recent sector development)
- Day 10: Second follow-up — different angle (partnership pathway vs. funder focus)
- Day 18: Break-up message — short, direct, leave the door open

If they reply at any point → route to `client-intelligence` skill to prep for the conversation.
